Ireland's cooling story is the data-centre story: server heat is rejected at a near-constant rate all year, while the island's heat demand follows the weather – the chart shows both shapes with annual totals matched. The winter deficit and summer surplus are the same energy in the wrong season, and moving heat across seasons is what aquifer thermal energy storage exists to do – charge the aquifer with summer rejection, discharge it into the winter peak. Tallaght has proven the loop since 2023, boosting data-centre heat to 80 °C for public buildings and homes; the Heat Bill advanced by Cabinet this week builds the framework, and the EU Energy Efficiency Directive already requires facilities above 1 MW to assess and implement waste-heat recovery unless certified infeasible. The heat is there, the mandate is arriving – the missing infrastructure is seasonal.

The chart prices the what-if strip in hardware, in installed thermal megawatts: the solid green base of each island bar is ground-source capacity operating today; the pale stack is what serving 20% of delivered buildings heat requires at 2,000 equivalent full-load hours†. Today's entire island stock is the sliver at the base – NI's barely renders. The three bars on the right are not scenarios but installed reality elsewhere: Sweden's ground-source fleet alone is nearly twice the island's whole what-if requirement, while the Netherlands and France add a second layer the island has none of – deeper geothermal, the Dutch doublet fleet and the Paris Basin networks†. NI needs the most new capacity relative to its size precisely because it is the most oil-heated; its documented track record above 60 kW – every system named, dated and statused in data.json, including one never commissioned through a design error and one running at 60% through an installation error – totals ≈0.6 MWth†. ROI's market grew fast to 2009, stalled, and is now 85% residential with ground-source at 4% of heat-pump sales. Method & sources

Annual buildings-heat anchors – SEAI Energy in Ireland 2025 and National Energy Balance (ROI); DfE Energy in Northern Ireland and NISRA Continuous Household Survey (NI) – are shaped by each week's population-weighted heating degree days (ERA5 via Open-Meteo). Gas gets a live regression where daily data exists; oil, the island's majority fuel, has no daily data anywhere – it is carried as an anchor plus a live price.
